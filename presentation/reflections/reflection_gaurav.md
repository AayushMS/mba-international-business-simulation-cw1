Before this assignment, I thought strategic goals were the easy part. You figure out what a company wants, write it down clearly, and move on. Working on Mobilé Inc. showed me how wrong that was. Writing goals for a company that earns $4.8 billion from a product under price pressure, has zero presence in 5G despite 70% urban coverage, and must fund AI R&D for a 2027-28 horizon is genuinely difficult. The three-wave tension shaped everything our team did, and honestly, it took me weeks to appreciate how interconnected all four tasks were.

My role was supporting Bishwesh on strategic goals and SMART objectives. Our first drafts were, to be fair, generic. I wrote things like "become a leading 5G player" and "invest in future technologies." Bishwesh pushed back and asked me to try removing "Mobilé Inc." from each goal to see if it still applied to Samsung or anyone else. Almost all of them did, which meant they failed as case-specific goals. The rewrite forced me to ground everything in actual data. A 5G goal was not useful unless it acknowledged that Mobilé has not launched a single 5G handset while 38% of Americans already own one. A financial discipline goal was empty without referencing the $90M cash floor and the gap between 4.8% normal and 7.8% emergency borrowing. Working with Bishwesh taught me that specificity is the difference between a document that sounds professional and one that actually guides decisions.

Applying the SMART criteria from Doran (1981) was harder than expected. Whether a 5G launch within eighteen months is "Achievable" depends on the strategic path: licensing is faster but sacrifices IP, while in-house R&D preserves ownership but takes longer. So our objectives had to be realistic under multiple scenarios. Professor Pradhananga's Week 1 lecture on "What is Strategy" covered how direction cascades from vision to measurable targets, and that cascade only works if each level is internally consistent. Our SMART objectives needed to hold regardless of which path Task 4 recommended.

That said, my own task accounts for maybe 30% of what I learned. The rest came from watching the other tasks unfold.

Bishan's VRIO analysis was a turning point. He found that Mobilé holds no sustained competitive advantages. The Atlanta manufacturing learning curve and distribution networks were only temporary advantages; most resources sat at competitive parity. I remember feeling a bit deflated reading his draft, because our goals had assumed Mobilé could build on existing strengths. But the VRIO showed there was not much to build on. Johnson et al. (2017) discuss the distinction between resource exploitation and resource creation, and Bishan's findings made that concrete. Our goals had to shift from "protect what we have" to "build what we lack," particularly around 5G capability.

Aayush's PESTLE and Porter's Five Forces analysis opened my eyes to external pressure. I had been thinking from an internal perspective, but Aayush showed the environment is not waiting. 5G infrastructure is built. Consumer demand for AI use cases is accelerating beyond forecasts. And post-DOJ, the oligopoly has shifted from tacit coordination to open rivalry. His point about competitive rivalry stuck with me: when four identical firms race toward 5G simultaneously, whoever breaks from the pack first gains a structural advantage the others struggle to close. That first-mover logic became central to our recommendation and made me reconsider our goals. Speed was not just preferable; it was strategically necessary.

The SWOT synthesis from Ruchan and Shaswat in Tasks 3 and 4 brought everything together. Ruchan mapped the gap between internal weaknesses (no 5G product, temporary advantages only) and external opportunities (ready infrastructure, open competitive window). That gap was the project's most critical finding. The team's final recommendation, accelerated 5G entry via licensing plus parallel AI R&D and managed 4G transition, directly responded to it. Shaswat's trade-off analysis made the recommendation credible by being honest about sacrifices: licensing means giving up IP control, and AI spending compresses margins while the $90M floor limits investment aggressiveness.

Looking back, I think the Baghai, Coley and White (1999) Three Horizons model best captures this case. Mobilé manages three horizons at once, all competing for the same finite money. Most textbook companies have a stable Horizon 1 funding the others. Mobilé's Horizon 1 is actively deteriorating as DOJ-triggered price competition squeezes 4G margins. That makes the resource allocation problem genuinely hard, not just theoretically interesting. Professor Pradhananga's Week 5 lecture on business strategy reinforced this when discussing how strategic choices must account for resource constraints across time horizons.

If I did this again, I would get involved in Task 2 earlier. We wrote goals before Bishan and Aayush finished their analyses, then had to revise once VRIO and PESTLE findings arrived. Starting with preliminary Task 2 outputs would have saved a full rewrite cycle. I also wish I had pushed for more market-specific goals, since the US, Europe, and Asia present very different competitive contexts.

Honestly, this was one of those assignments where the process taught me more than any single framework. Strategy is not linear. The analysis reshapes the goals, and the goals shape what you analyse. That iterative quality was something I had read about in Johnson et al. (2017) but never experienced until this project. Seeing six people with different analytical lenses converge on the same conclusion gave me a stronger sense of what strategic thinking looks like in practice.
