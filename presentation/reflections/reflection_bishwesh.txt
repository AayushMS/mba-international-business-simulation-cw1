I want to start by admitting something. When our group divided the tasks, I assumed being Strategy Lead on Task 1 would be the easiest job. Vision, mission, goals, SMART objectives. I had done this before. Looking back, I was wrong on two counts: the task itself was harder than expected, and my own task turned out to be the smallest part of what I actually learned from this project.

The Mobile case is not a clean strategy problem. The company sits at the intersection of three technology waves that all demand resources simultaneously, funded from one pool of money. Professor Pradhananga introduced this tension in her Week 1 lecture on "What is Strategy," discussing how strategy is fundamentally about choices under constraint. I understood that in class, but I did not truly feel it until we tried writing a vision statement that had to acknowledge a $4.8 billion cash engine in decline, a 5G product that does not exist, and an AI future three years away. Fitting all of that into one directional statement was honestly painful.

Gaurav and I collaborated on the goals and SMART objectives. Our first drafts were embarrassing, things like "become a leading smartphone innovator" that could describe any tech company. Gaurav caught it first: none of our drafts mentioned 5G specifically. We scrapped everything and rebuilt around the three-wave structure. I set a personal test: if you could swap "Mobile" for any other company name and the goal still made sense, it was too generic.

The $90M cash reserve floor shaped the SMART objectives heavily. Gaurav argued that financial discipline was itself strategic, not just a constraint, because breaching the floor triggers 7.8% emergency borrowing versus the normal 4.8%, and that premium signals distress to investors. I came around to his view. That reframing stayed in the final version.

But honestly, the parts of this project that changed how I think came from watching my teammates' work and seeing how the whole analysis connected.

Bishan's VRIO findings hit me hard. Mobile holds no sustained competitive advantages, only temporary ones. My first reaction was that it undermined the vision I had written. How do you promise technology leadership when your internal analysis says there is no durable edge? Atlanta's learning curve only applies to 4G production, as Bishan showed using Barney's (1991) criteria. Vietnam's low labour costs are geographical, not proprietary, since Samsung and LG operate there too. This was uncomfortable, but it was the honest assessment Professor Pradhananga pushed us toward in her Week 3 lecture on internal analysis.

Aayush's external work added another layer. His Porter's Five Forces showed rivalry intensifying across four identical firms, all racing toward 5G after the DOJ ended tacit price coordination. His key insight: whoever establishes 5G brand leadership first gains a structural advantage others will struggle to close. His PESTLE analysis also reframed something I had wrong. I assumed 5G was a future opportunity. Aayush showed the infrastructure is already built, with 70% US urban coverage and 65% penetration in China. The market is waiting for Mobile, not the other way around.

Everything clicked when Ruchan presented the SWOT synthesis. He did not just list strengths and weaknesses. He mapped connections across quadrants, showing how weaknesses combined with threats to create urgent problems while strengths aligned with opportunities to create actionable paths. I remember thinking it was the first time I had seen a SWOT do something useful beyond being a textbook matrix. Shaswat then grounded the recommendation in financial reality, constantly asking whether we could afford what we were proposing. Johnson et al. (2017) describe strategic evaluation through suitability, acceptability, and feasibility. Shaswat was our feasibility check, and his work on the Vietnam capacity decision was the practical thinking that separates a recommendation from a wish list.

The executive summary was the last thing I wrote, and it nearly broke me. Professor Pradhananga's Week 5 lecture on business strategy covered how strategic choices need to be communicated crisply. If you cannot summarise your strategy in a few sentences, you probably do not understand it. Baghai, Coley and White's (1999) Three Horizons framework helped me structure the summary. Their argument that successful companies invest across all horizons simultaneously, not sequentially, maps directly onto Mobile's challenge. The critical difference is that Mobile's Horizon 1 is actively eroding under post-DOJ competition, so the usual assumption of a stable cash engine funding future growth does not hold.

If I could redo this project, I would start Task 1 later. We drafted vision and mission before the Task 2 analyses were complete, then had to revise once we had the full picture. Starting with analysis first would have produced stronger drafts and saved us a round of rework.

To be fair, this project taught me more about strategy than any single lecture could. Strategy is not about picking a direction and running. It is about understanding what you cannot do, being honest about what you lack, and making trade-offs explicit rather than hidden. I came in thinking my job was to write an inspiring vision. I leave understanding that a vision only matters if the analysis behind it is rigorous and the team is willing to tell you when you are being too vague. I was lucky to have teammates who did exactly that.
