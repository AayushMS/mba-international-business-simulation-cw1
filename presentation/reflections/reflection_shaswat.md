Before this project, I had never thought of myself as the person who tells people what they cannot do. But that is essentially what my role became. Ruchan led the strategic recommendation for Task 4, and I supported him on financial feasibility, risk assessment, and trade-off analysis. In practice, that meant running every ambitious idea through the filter of whether Mobilé could actually afford it without breaching the $90M cash reserve floor.

My contribution only makes sense in the context of the broader project. Bishwesh and Gaurav shaped the vision and mission in Task 1, and honestly, that set the tone for everything. A good vision acts as a filter for resource allocation. Professor Pradhananga made a similar point in her Week 5 lecture when she discussed how strategic choices flow from a clear sense of purpose. When I evaluated trade-offs in Task 4, I kept going back to the strategic intent they had defined.

The Task 2 framework analyses gave me the data I needed. Bishan's VRIO finding that Mobilé holds no sustained competitive advantages was sobering. But his Value Chain work gave me something concrete: Atlanta at $2.9 million per line with $28/hr labour, Vietnam at $3.3 million per line but only $6/hr. Those numbers determined how much each capacity expansion would cost. Aayush's PESTLE and Porter's analyses showed the external pressures: 5G adoption at 38% in the US, four identical firms about to compete aggressively post-DOJ. The urgency was real, but so were the financial limits. When Ruchan pulled everything into the SWOT for Task 3, the recommendation's shape became clear. The critical weakness (no 5G handset) meeting the critical opportunity (infrastructure ready, demand proven) pointed toward accelerated 5G entry. But "accelerated" costs money, and that is where my work started.

The trade-off I spent the most time on was the 5G technology decision: build in-house or license. Ruchan's strategic vision was bold, and my job was to make sure it was feasible. He favoured in-house R&D because it builds proprietary IP, a path to sustained advantage under VRIO. I understood the logic. But the timing did not work. Every quarter of delay means competitors locking in carrier partnerships and brand loyalty. Grant (2019) discusses how adoption follows an S-curve: enter during rapid acceleration, you catch the wave; enter during the plateau, you fight established players on price. I argued for licensing with a parallel in-house R&D track for the next generation. To be fair, this is a compromise, and I wanted us to be honest about that rather than pretending it was ideal.

The $90M cash floor was the constraint I kept coming back to. I think some teammates found it frustrating, but that floor is not just a financial rule. It is strategic discipline. Teece et al. (1997) write about dynamic capabilities as the ability to reconfigure resources as the environment shifts. You cannot reconfigure anything without financial headroom. Emergency borrowing at 7.8% versus 4.8% is a 62% cost premium on debt, and triggering it signals to investors that management has lost control. Honestly, framing the cash floor as a strategic asset rather than just a limitation was one of the more useful mental shifts I made during this project.

Production capacity was where I felt I contributed the most original thinking. New lines take two years from investment to operation. Investing in Vietnam expansion in 2025 means capacity arrives in 2027, right when 5G demand peaks and AI devices reach commercial launch. But committing under demand uncertainty is risky: overproduction carries a 5-10% cost penalty, underproduction means permanently lost sales under JIT rules. Ferdows (1997) describes a spectrum of foreign factory roles from basic offshore to strategic lead plants. Vietnam is currently just an offshore cost play, but expanded capacity could push it toward something more valuable. I argued for investing now. The cost of permanently missing a market outweighs a temporary overproduction penalty, and when risks are asymmetric the decision gets clearer.

Bishan's Value Chain analysis gave me the production cost data I needed to make this argument concrete. Aayush's PESTLE work on adoption rates gave me the demand-side evidence. I could not have built the trade-off analysis without their findings.

Working with Ruchan on the final recommendation was a good experience. There were moments of tension, particularly when he wanted more aggressive AI R&D spending and I kept pointing at the cash floor. But that tension produced a better outcome. He came up with the phrase "Funded by Managed 4G Transition" to make the financial mechanism part of the strategic headline. I thought that was smart because it signalled the team had thought about how to pay for its ambitions, not just what they were.

If I could change one thing, I would have pushed earlier for a shared financial framework before individual analyses began. Each person worked with slightly different assumptions about how much headroom Mobilé has, and reconciling those in Task 4 took time we could have saved.

This project taught me that the person handling trade-offs is not the pessimist in the group. They are the one making sure the strategy is real. Grant (2019) argues that strategy without resource grounding is aspiration, not strategy. I think he is right. The hardest part was not the financial modelling. It was telling teammates "we cannot afford that" and then helping find what we could afford instead.
