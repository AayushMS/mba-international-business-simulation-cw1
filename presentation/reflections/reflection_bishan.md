Going into this project, I expected internal analysis to be the most straightforward part. I had studied VRIO and Value Chain in previous modules, and the case study gave us plenty of data on resources and operations. What I did not expect was that my findings would become one of the more uncomfortable truths the group had to sit with. Honestly, the moment I realised Mobile has no sustained competitive advantages was also the moment the project stopped feeling like an academic exercise and started feeling like a real strategic problem.

My VRIO analysis was the first major deliverable I completed. I listed every resource from the case and ran each through Barney's (1991) four criteria. Atlanta's learning-curve efficiencies seemed like a strong candidate for sustained advantage, and I had written it up that way initially. But those efficiencies apply to 4G manufacturing only. For 5G, every firm starts from a similar baseline. Vietnam was similar. On the surface, $6/hr versus $28/hr looks like a clear cost advantage, but Samsung, LG, and Intel all operate in Vietnam too. The cost advantage is geographical, not proprietary. I remember wanting to present a more optimistic picture, and Aayush pushed me not to soften it. He said an honest assessment would serve the rest of the analysis better than a generous one. He was right, because the accuracy of my findings directly affected what Ruchan could do with the SWOT.

The Value Chain work produced what I think was my strongest contribution: identifying the 2-year production capacity lag as a hard constraint. If Mobile commits to new Vietnam lines in 2025, they come online in 2027, exactly when 5G demand peaks and AI-devices reach commercial viability. Under JIT rules, underproduction means permanently lost sales. Porter (1985) treats technology development as a support activity, but for Mobile, R&D determines whether there is a product to manufacture and sell in the 5G wave at all.

But my part was only one piece. What made this project work was how the tasks connected.

Bishwesh and Gaurav's Task 1 work on vision and strategic objectives set the direction for everything. When Bishwesh presented the vision, I remember thinking it was ambitious given what I was finding internally. There was genuine tension between the aspirational goals and the resource gaps I was uncovering. That tension turned out to be productive, forcing the recommendation to address the gap between aspiration and reality.

Aayush's external analysis complemented mine in ways I had not anticipated. His PESTLE finding that 5G infrastructure already covers 70%+ of US urban areas connected directly to my production lag observation. The external environment is ready and waiting, but the internal machinery needs two years to retool. Neither of us had seen that connection working alone. His Porter's Five Forces work also gave context to my VRIO results. Temporary advantages in a high-rivalry environment where four equal firms are racing toward 5G simultaneously is not just a weakness on a framework chart. It is a strategic warning.

When Ruchan took my findings into the SWOT synthesis, I could see how the discipline of not overstating strengths paid off. He did not just copy my results into the strength column. He mapped cross-links, showing how Mobile's dual-plant structure connected to Asia's growth opportunity but was undermined by Vietnam's immaturity and tariff risk. I think I learned more about the strategic value of honest internal assessment by watching how Ruchan used my work than I did from producing it.

The Task 4 recommendation, which Ruchan and Shaswat shaped, built directly on constraints I had identified. The decision to recommend licensing 5G technology rather than developing it in-house was partly a response to my finding that R&D capability was only a potential advantage and would need years to become real. Shaswat's financial analysis of the $90M cash floor added urgency I had not fully appreciated from the internal side alone.

Professor Pradhananga's Week 3 lecture on internal analysis shaped how I approached VRIO. She stressed that the purpose is not to list strengths but to honestly assess which resources generate competitive advantage. I kept coming back to that when I was tempted to be generous. The Week 5 lecture on business strategy helped me understand why accuracy matters for downstream tasks. If I overstate a strength, it carries through the SWOT and distorts the recommendation. Grant (2019) reinforced this by arguing that competitive advantage comes from resources that are both scarce and relevant to the competitive environment, and Mobile's most relevant resource turned out to be one it does not yet possess.

If I were doing this again, I would start narrower. My first pass tried to evaluate everything and spread the analysis too thin. Going deep on three or four strategically important resources from the start would have been more efficient and more useful to the team.

I think the biggest thing this project taught me is that good analysis sometimes means delivering findings nobody wants to hear. The honest answer for Mobile is that its internal position is weaker than its revenue figures suggest. That was hard to write and hard to present. But because the team treated it as a starting point rather than a verdict, it became the foundation for a recommendation that actually addressed the real situation. I am genuinely proud of how we handled that.
