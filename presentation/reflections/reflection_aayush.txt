When I first read the Mobile Inc. case, I thought the company was in a reasonable spot: $4.8 billion in 4G revenue, 25% market share, plants on two continents. It took our group discussions to help me see how fragile that position actually was. The three-wave tension (4G cash cow under price pressure, 5G urgently needed, AI on the horizon) is the kind of strategic dilemma that does not have a clean answer, and sitting with it for weeks made me appreciate how hard these decisions are when money and time are both limited.

My role was the external analysis: PESTLE and Porter's Five Forces. I started with PESTLE, and honestly, my first attempt was embarrassing. Generic factors like "government regulation" and "economic uncertainty" that you could paste into any case. The moment it clicked was when I asked one question for each factor: what does this specifically mean for Mobile's ability to manage all three waves at once? That filter killed the noise. The DOJ antitrust intervention in early 2025 became the single most important factor, because it ended the tacit price coordination that had kept all four firms comfortable. Suddenly it was open competition, and Mobile had no 5G product to compete with.

The technological factor surprised me most. I had assumed Mobile's big problem was that 5G networks were not ready yet. Wrong. The GSMA (2024) data showed that 5G coverage already exceeds 70% of US urban areas, Europe's major cities are connected, and China has 65% 5G penetration. The infrastructure is there, waiting. Mobile just has nothing to sell into it. Framing it that way changed my whole analysis.

For Porter's Five Forces, I spent the most time on competitive rivalry and rated it high and intensifying. Four equally-sized firms, high exit barriers from manufacturing sunk costs, all of them racing toward 5G and AI at the same time. Porter (1980) talks about choosing a defensible competitive position rather than trying to be everything to everyone, and that kept me grounded. The practical implication was clear: whoever breaks from the pack first on 5G gains a structural advantage the others will struggle to close. That first-mover insight became a load-bearing piece of our final recommendation.

But what I am most proud of in this project is not my individual frameworks. It is how the analysis came together as a team.

Working alongside Bishan was genuinely one of the better experiences. He was doing the internal analysis (VRIO and Value Chain) while I handled the external side. His Value Chain work uncovered the 2-year production capacity lag. When he told me that, I immediately connected it to my PESTLE findings: if 5G infrastructure is already built and demand is proven, that production investment cannot wait. His internal constraint and my external opportunity pointed to the same conclusion. Neither of us would have seen the full picture alone.

Ruchan did an amazing job pulling everything together for the SWOT and the recommendation. He could not really start until Bishan and I had finished, which must have been stressful. The discipline he brought to the SWOT, requiring every point to trace back to a specific framework finding, forced me to make sure my analyses were precise enough to support that synthesis. The strongest part was how the SWOT showed that Mobile's 5G gap appeared across four frameworks simultaneously: my PESTLE, my Porter's, Bishan's VRIO, and his Value Chain. Ruchan wove it into a single point that carried the weight of all four.

Bishwesh's work on the vision and mission took on a different meaning for me after completing the external analysis. Before PESTLE and Porter's, the vision sounded aspirational in a normal way. After seeing the competitive reality, I understood why it needed to be specific about 5G and AI rather than something vague like "be a global leader." The gap between where Mobile is and where it needs to be made a generic vision meaningless.

Professor Pradhananga's Week 7 lecture on global and collaborative strategies was directly relevant. Bartlett and Ghoshal's framework helped me see that Mobile cannot treat its three markets the same way. The US rewards brand positioning, Europe values features and sustainability, Asia is split between premium 5G and mass-market 4G. Three separate strategies would be too expensive given the $90M cash floor, but a single global approach would miss each market's logic. The answer is a "transnational" approach (Bartlett and Ghoshal's term), standardising some capabilities globally while adapting execution locally. Johnson et al. (2017) also shaped how I structured the Five Forces, especially their emphasis on using it to understand overall profit potential rather than as a checklist.

One thing I would do differently is spend more time on supplier power. The AI chip sourcing question could become a real constraint if Qualcomm, Apple, and Samsung vertically integrate. I flagged it but did not develop it enough. Coming from Nepal, where I have seen firsthand how supply chain bottlenecks affect much smaller industries, I should have trusted that instinct.

This project taught me that external analysis is not about listing environmental factors. It is about understanding how the external world constrains and enables what a company can actually do, then connecting that back to internal resources. The bridge between my frameworks and Bishan's internal analysis is where the real strategic insight lives. I will carry that forward.
